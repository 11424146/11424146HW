## Topic 2: Terminal Velocity of a Parachute

### 1. Problem

When an object falls in air, its speed does not increase forever.  
At first, it gets faster quickly.  
Then air resistance becomes stronger, so the speed grows slowly.  
In the end, the speed is close to a fixed value. This is terminal velocity.

Goal of this report:  
build an equation for speed, solve it, and explain terminal velocity.

---

### 2. Assumptions

To make the problem easier, we use these assumptions:

1. We only study up-down motion (downward is positive).  
2. Gravity is constant: `mg`.  
3. Air resistance is proportional to speed: `F_d = kv` (upward).  
4. `m`, `g`, and `k` are constants.  
5. Initial speed is `v(0)=0` (start from rest).

---

### 3. Differential Equation

From Newton's second law:

$$
m\frac{dv}{dt} = mg - kv
$$

We get a first-order linear ODE:

$$
\frac{dv}{dt} + \frac{k}{m}v = g
$$

---

### 4. Solution

The solution is:

$$
v(t) = \frac{mg}{k} + Ce^{-\frac{k}{m}t}
$$

Use initial condition `v(0)=0`:

$$
0 = \frac{mg}{k} + C \Rightarrow C = -\frac{mg}{k}
$$

So we have:

$$
v(t)=\frac{mg}{k}\left(1-e^{-\frac{k}{m}t}\right)
$$

---

### 5. Meaning of the Result

1. When `t → ∞`, `e^{-kt/m} → 0`, so

$$
\lim_{t\to\infty}v(t)=\frac{mg}{k}
$$

This is the **terminal velocity**.

2. After some time, gravity and air resistance are balanced, so speed does not increase.  

3. From the formula:
   - Bigger `m` usually means bigger terminal velocity.  
   - Bigger `k` (stronger drag, for example a bigger parachute) means smaller terminal velocity, so it is safer.

---

### 6. Example

Assume:

- `m=80 kg`
- `g=9.8 m/s^2`
- `k=40 N·s/m`

Then the terminal velocity is

$$
v_T=\frac{mg}{k}=\frac{80\times 9.8}{40}=19.6\text{ m/s}
$$

The speed function is

$$
v(t)=19.6\left(1-e^{-0.5t}\right)\text{ m/s}
$$

Explanation:  
The speed rises fast at first, then slowly goes to `19.6 m/s`.

---

### 7. Conclusion

This first-order differential equation can describe the falling process well.  
Speed is fast at first, then slower, and finally almost constant.  
In engineering, this model helps us think about parachute safety and design.  
Real cases can be more complex (drag may depend on `v^2`), but this model is good for basic analysis.
